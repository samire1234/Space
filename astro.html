AMERICA BULLETIN

NASA launched the three-astronaut Apollo space expedition program after the one-astronaut Mercury and two-astronaut Gemini space expedition programs. The program, whose preparations began during the reign of President Eisenhower, turned into a manned mission to the Moon. The first manned journey to the Moon took place on December 21, 1968, with the Apollo 8 space expedition. Frank Borman, James Lovell and William Anders were both the first to travel to the Moon and the first to leave Earth's low orbit and enter the orbit of another celestial body (the Moon). They became people. The Apollo 8 team reached the Moon in 68 hours and successfully returned to Earth orbit after making 10 trips around the Moon for 21 hours.

Between December 21, 1968 and December 19, 1972, 24 astronauts traveled to the Moon in 9 expeditions organized by NASA. In six of these expeditions, one astronaut remained in the spacecraft, while the other two astronauts landed on the Moon with the module. They moonwalked. When the first man set foot on the Moon, calendars on Earth showed July 20, 1969. The last time a human stood on the Moon was December 13, 1972.
